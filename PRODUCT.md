# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The product serves two overlapping F1 audiences. The primary product user is an engaged fan who follows sessions live, compares race data, reviews analytics, plays Fantasy Pitwall, and wants tactical context without leaving the site. A secondary but important user is the casual fan who wants a clean way to read news, check standings, browse photos, follow the schedule, and understand the championship without needing specialist knowledge.

Users may arrive during a race weekend, between sessions, or after a Grand Prix. They need fast orientation, reliable data, and a visual system that feels connected to Formula 1 without making every screen feel like a broadcast graphic.

## Product Purpose

F1 Paddock Insider combines Formula 1 editorial content, live timing, analytics, replay, media, standings, schedule, and Fantasy Pitwall into a single multilingual experience. It should make casual browsing feel immediate and polished while giving committed fans enough operational depth to inspect performance, strategy, telemetry, and fantasy outcomes.

Success means users can move from a news story to standings, from a live session to analytics, or from a race result to fantasy scoring without losing context. The product should feel current, data-backed, and race-aware.

## Brand Personality

Precise, energetic, and informed.

The tone should carry the urgency and sharpness of a race weekend, but stay readable and trustworthy. It can feel premium and motorsport-native, but it should not become loud for its own sake. Technical screens should feel like a serious race desk; editorial screens should feel like a confident paddock publication.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic sports-news templates with interchangeable article grids, ad-heavy composition, and weak visual hierarchy.

Avoid beige or blue SaaS dashboards, finance-style executive reporting, and generic admin-panel styling that disconnects the product from motorsport.

Avoid excessive gamer or neon aesthetics, decorative cyberpunk effects, and motion that competes with data readability.

Avoid UI decoration that slows down live-session comprehension, especially oversized cards, repeated icon-card grids, ornamental gradients, glass effects, and visual noise around timing tables or analytics charts.

## Design Principles

1. Put the race context first. Every surface should quickly answer what session, round, driver, team, or championship state the user is looking at.

2. Let depth unfold progressively. Casual fans should understand the page immediately, while advanced fans can drill into timing, telemetry, strategy, scoring, and replay without switching tools.

3. Treat data as live operational material. Tables, charts, banners, and status indicators should prioritize scanning, comparison, freshness, and confidence over decoration.

4. Keep motorsport energy under control. Use Formula 1 cues, speed, contrast, and team color deliberately, but preserve legibility and task flow.

5. Make editorial and product surfaces feel related. News, galleries, standings, analytics, live timing, replay, and fantasy should share a coherent system even when their density changes.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target practical WCAG AA behavior for contrast, focus visibility, keyboard access, tap target size, and readable responsive layouts.

Support casual readers and expert users through clear labels, predictable navigation, multilingual copy, and progressive disclosure of technical detail. Do not rely on color alone for timing states, chart meaning, fantasy outcomes, or errors. Motion should communicate state and remain limited enough to respect reduced-motion preferences.
